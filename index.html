Heritage Hijacked: Chinese Sword Dance at the SensoNet Culture War Centre.

Dispute over unauthorised sensory capture reignites global debate on cultural ownership and exploitation in the age of sensory media technology.

Zoe Leung August 12 2025

A western influencer ignited a significant controversy by downloading, remixing, and uploading a traditional Chinese sword dance, a traditional dance in China, on the sensor reality platform SensoNet. The unauthorised remix immediately became popular on Western SensoNet servers, resulting in millions of views and commercial income. However, the dancers who first presented the dance said that their cultural heritage had been stolen of its meaning, resulting in harsh condemnation of digital colonialism.

Digital Colonialism: The exploitation of culture and identity through digital platforms, where traditional knowledge is repackaged without consent for commercial gain.

The first SensoCap posted by the Chinese dancer allowed the other users to feel the pride of demonstrating this martial arts inspired dance performance. However, the viral remix Blade Beats presented only the physical movements while overlaying them with flashing neon effects, synthetic sound effects, and artificially heightened emotional triggers. What began as a controlled cultural exhibition has evolved into a tremendous entertainment event.

The dance performance from Lisa Xu's SensoNet account

Voices from China

In reaction, a large crowd of traditional dancers gathered outside the Chinese Cultural Performance Centre to show their rage. Lisa Xu the first performer of the original SensoCap, revealed her response upon witnessing the changed version.

Cultural historian Zhou Xuewen raised similar concerns, pointing out that this occurrence is part of a larger trend of cultural abuse:

It's not the first time. A few years ago, some foreign influencer recreated Chinese users’ work without permission. However, it is becoming increasingly aggressive. They benefit from it while stripping it of its significance. It is stealing.
Online outrage and policy flaws

The controversial post by Lisa Xu on the remix of SensoNet quickly went viral, resulting in global social media protests. As activists and cultural advocates criticised the platform's lax protections for cultural consent, hashtags including #StopSensoryColonization and #RespectCulture gained traction throughout the world.

At the core of the problem is SensoNet's cultural consent mechanism, which theoretically requires permission from cultural institutions before remixing protected sensory data. However, critics claim that the policy contains considerable gaps. The main issue is how the platform defines "derivative use." If a remix changes more than 40% of the original sensoCap, it is classified as a derivative work and does not require further permission. According to SensoNet, Blade Beats met this criterion, indicating that the foreign influencer's version conforms with platform legal requirements.

Amy Jahan, a global cultural rights campaigner, said

A new frontier of colonialism. People's heritage, memories, customs, and emotions are being marketed as exotic commodities, rather than land or labour. The platform gains, while communities lose everything. SensoNet's present permission method is a flimsy barrier that only protects the company, not the culture.
SensoNet Response

SensoNet responded to mounting outrage by stating that remixed content aligns with their current transformative use policy. SensoNet said they are trying to achieve a balance between cultural respect and creative freedom.

The comment generated more outrage, with accusing the firm of putting money ahead of cultural integrity. Several Chinese cultural institutes then made official demands for suspension. However, because the remix originated outside of China, international enforcement is still dubious.

Broader implications

According to other experts, the conflict raises larger questions about cultural ownership in the SensoNet world. When SensoNet first appeared, it was lauded as a breakthrough tool for preserving and sharing traditions, particularly among diaspora populations looking to reconnect with their roots. However, as sensory data has become a viable commercial commodity, there are growing worries that heritage is being repackaged as entertainment without regard for the people that developed it.

The argument about Blade Beats emphasises the conflict between creative freedom and cultural reverence. Advocates fear that without better global protections, such instances would continue to decontextualize indigenous behaviours and generate money for outsiders.

Lisa Xu's Next Steps

Lisa Xu and her fellow traditional dancers have started a global petition to push for tougher border cultural permission legislation, greater sensory data protection. Xu said,

We are not opposed to sharing our culture. However, sharing is not soteling. If anyone wants to remix our work, they should ask us first, acknowledge its significance, and contribute back to our community.

The organisation also intends to meet with cultural authorities in Beijing to push for an international accord on cultural sensory rights. Their battle has become a symbol of a bigger discussion over who gains and who loses out when cultural memory is transformed into statistics.

Cultural activists believe that as the petition gathers global signatures, this dispute will serve as a watershed moment in how global platforms treat intangible property. It is unclear whether SensoNet will amend its practices, but this case has generated rising requests for stronger regulations to guarantee cultural traditions are shared legitimately rather than abused for profit.
